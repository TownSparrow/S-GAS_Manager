[Chunk 001] Shar is one of the oldest and most powerful deities in the Forgotten Realms pantheon. She is the goddess of darkness, loss, secrets, and the void. She was born alongside her twin sister Selune at the dawn of creation, before gods walked the land and before mortals drew breath. Shar embodies the primordial void — the endless silence that existed before light was ever kindled. Her domain covers not just physical darkness but the darkness of forgotten memories, buried grief, and the weight of secrets kept at great personal cost. Her church, the Night Masks, operates across Faerun.

[Chunk 002] In Baldur's Gate 3, Shar serves as the ultimate antagonist behind the game's central conflict. She orchestrates a grand scheme to extend the Shadow-Cursed Lands across all of Faerun, seeking to plunge the world into eternal night. Unlike simpler villains, Shar does not act with brute force alone; she manipulates her followers through sacred oaths, memory erasure, and divine promises. Her plan involves the Absolute — a false deity construct — and the use of the Netherstones to control the mind flayer tadpoles implanted in the player and key NPCs. Shar's reach extends from the Underdark to the heavens.

[Chunk 003] The eternal rivalry between Shar and Selune is the cosmological bedrock of Baldur's Gate 3's narrative. According to Faerunian myth, when the universe was young, Shar and Selune shared power equally. But Selune reached into the primordial fire and created the sun, introducing light into a universe Shar preferred dark. In retaliation, Shar struck Selune, and from the blood of that wound, the goddess Mystryl — later Mystra — was born. This fracture became a permanent war. Neither sister can truly destroy the other, as they are two sides of the same cosmic coin, forever locked in opposition.

[Chunk 004] Selune is the goddess of the moon, stars, travellers, and those who sail by night. She is Shar's twin and eternal opposite. Where Shar rules the darkness between stars, Selune rules the light of stars themselves. Her clergy, the Moonguard, are sworn to protect travellers, fight undead, and bring hope to the lost. In Baldur's Gate 3, Selune's presence is felt through Shadowheart's suppressed memories, the Moonrise Towers' corrupted name, and the Grove's quiet moonlit sanctuaries. Selune represents change, hope, and the idea that even in deepest darkness, a distant light still shines to guide the way home.

[Chunk 005] The conflict between Shar and Selune has shaped Faerun's history for millennia. Ancient texts describe wars in the heavens where divine armies clashed over territories both literal and spiritual. On the mortal plane, this manifests as cultural tensions between those who worship darkness and those who revere the moon. In Baldur's Gate 3, Act Two takes place almost entirely within the Shadow-Cursed Lands — a region where this divine war has left its most visible scar. Villages stand empty, crops have blackened, and creatures that once served life now serve only shadow. The land itself mourns the absence of light.

[Chunk 006] Shar's influence is not merely destructive — it is seductive. She promises peace through forgetfulness: the suffering you cannot recall cannot hurt you. This doctrine attracts broken people, traumatised soldiers, and those too exhausted to carry their grief any longer. In Baldur's Gate 3, this is illustrated powerfully through Shadowheart, who willingly submitted to memory erasure in Shar's service. The Dark Lady promises her faithful a quiet oblivion, a numbing of all pain. Shar does not demand joy — she only asks for surrender. Her followers call this peace the "Dark Gift," and many consider it a mercy rather than a curse.

[Chunk 007] The Netherstones are three sacred artifacts of immense power, originally carved from the Shadowfell and infused with Shar's divine essence. Each stone is linked to a specific domain: mind, shadow, and void. Together, they form the Crown of Karsus, a relic capable of controlling all mind flayer tadpoles simultaneously. In Baldur's Gate 3, the player character and companions all carry parasitic tadpoles implanted by the Nautiloid crash. The Netherstones can suppress or amplify the infection's neurological effects. Whoever assembles all three gains dominion over the True Soul network — an army of thralls bound by Shar's divine Absolute construct.

[Chunk 008] The Shadow-Cursed Lands are sustained by the Netherstones acting as anchors of dark magic. Without them, the curse would gradually fade. But as long as they remain active and wielded by Shar's chosen, the supernatural darkness persists and spreads. The three Netherstones were held by three generals of the Absolute: Ketheric Thorm, Enver Gortash, and Orin the Red. Each controls a different aspect of the Absolute's operations. Ketheric, resurrected by his pact with Shar, maintained the Shadow-Cursed Lands from Moonrise Towers. Destroying him is a prerequisite for lifting the shadow curse and allowing moonlight to return to the blighted region.

[Chunk 009] Mind flayer tadpoles are the central mechanic linking all major plot threads in Baldur's Gate 3. These parasites were implanted in the player character aboard the Nautiloid, a mind flayer ship that crashed at the game's opening. The tadpoles slowly transform their hosts into mind flayers, but the Netherstones interfere with this process, slowing the transformation when held by Shar's agents. The tadpoles also grant illithid powers — telepathy, domination, and limited psionic ability — which grow as the player embraces rather than resists the infection. This creates a constant moral tension: power at the cost of identity and alignment with Shar.

[Chunk 010] The Shadow-Cursed Lands dominate Act Two of Baldur's Gate 3 and represent Shar's most visible ongoing project. The region was once fertile farmland and prosperous villages surrounding Moonrise Towers. After Ketheric Thorm made his pact with Shar and lost his daughter Isobel, his grief became the seed of the curse. The magical darkness is not merely visual — it carries a spiritual weight that drains the will and corrupts the mind. Creatures caught in it without protection transform into shadow creatures. The curse radiates outward from Moonrise Towers, and players must either carry a Moon Lantern or travel with Isobel's blessing to move safely.

[Chunk 011] The narrative structure of Baldur's Gate 3 consistently forces players to confront the binary between Shar and Selune, darkness and light. Act One introduces the conflict subtly through Shadowheart and the missing Selunite artifact she carries. Act Two brings it to the forefront with the Shadow-Cursed Lands and Ketheric Thorm's history. Act Three resolves it with the assault on Baldur's Gate itself, where the assembled Netherstones give someone ultimate power over the Absolute. The game never simplifies the moral choice: embracing Shar's darkness is always tempting because the power is real, but the cost is measured in souls, memories, and freedom.

[Chunk 012] Moonrise Towers is the primary location of Act Two and serves as Shar's operational centre in the Shadow-Cursed Lands. The fortress was once a watchtower maintained by Selunite monks who used it to observe lunar cycles and chart the heavens. After Ketheric Thorm's fall and resurrection under Shar's pact, the towers were seized and repurposed as a prison and staging ground for the Absolute's operations. Hundreds of True Souls — Shar's branded agents — congregate there. The dungeons hold prisoners and mind flayer experimentation chambers. The tower's upper sanctum is where Ketheric Thorm himself resides, surrounded by shadows and undead loyal only to his grief-driven will.

[Chunk 013] Defending Moonrise Towers requires navigating layers of fanatical guards, Sharran cultists, and shadow creatures. Players must decide how to approach the fortress — infiltration, diplomacy with True Souls, or direct assault. The Absolute's power is strongest here: branded agents are immune to many conventional effects. Inside, players discover the True Soul network, a mind flayer hive-mind substitute created by the Netherstones. They also learn Ketheric Thorm was once a Selunite paladin of extraordinary devotion who turned to Shar only after losing everything. His arc is a cautionary tale about grief weaponised by a goddess of loss who profits from despair.

[Chunk 014] The Druid's Grove in Act One represents Selune's quiet sanctuary against encroaching darkness. It is home to a community of druids led by Kagha, Tiefling refugees fleeing Elturel's destruction, and a small band of adventurers. The grove is magically warded, and the druids are debating whether to seal it — locking out the Tieflings — to preserve their own safety. Selune's influence is felt in the moonlit clearings, the healing herbs, and the Selunite acolyte Nettie who tends the sick. The conflict at the Grove mirrors the larger divine war: it is a community being torn between isolationist self-preservation and communal hope.

[Chunk 015] Within the Druid's Grove, Shadowheart's personal arc begins to unravel. She carries a powerful Selunite artifact — the Mysterious Artefact — that she was tasked with delivering to Moonrise Towers for Shar. The irony is exquisite: a Sharran cleric carrying a Selunite holy relic into Shar's stronghold, with her memories erased to ensure compliance. The Grove's Selunite atmosphere visibly unsettles Shadowheart, triggering suppressed instincts and half-memories she cannot explain. Players who spend time in the Grove with her begin to see cracks in her Sharran conditioning. It is the first hint that her devotion to Shar was built on a foundation of stolen identity.

[Chunk 016] The pivotal choice between supporting Shar or Selune arrives most directly during Shadowheart's personal questline in Act Three. After recovering her true memories — including the fact that her biological parents are Selunite worshippers whom Shar's clergy imprisoned — Shadowheart must decide whether to execute them as Shar demands, or free them and renounce her goddess. If the player has guided her toward Selune throughout the journey, she is capable of choosing light. If aligned with Shar's path, she kills her parents and ascends as Shar's chosen. This moment crystallises the game's central theme: identity is not what you were made to be, but what you choose to be.

[Chunk 017] If Shadowheart remains devoted to Shar by the end, she becomes Shar's Dark Justiciar — a divine assassin blessed with shadow magic. This ending sees her memory fully restored but twisted: she knows who she was, and she still chooses darkness. She receives the Spear of Night and becomes one of Shar's most powerful champions. The ending is haunting precisely because it is not forced. It is a willing surrender, informed by full knowledge of her past. Shar rewards compliance with power, and for some players — and for Shadowheart — the power feels worth the cost. Darkness wins not through conquest but through consent.

[Chunk 018] When Shadowheart chooses Selune, she becomes a Paladin of Selune, her shadow magic replaced by radiant moonlight. Her parents are freed from their Sharran prison, and she undergoes a profound transformation — not a power boost, but a spiritual homecoming. She remembers who she was before the Sharran clergy erased her: a Selunite acolyte named Jenevelle Hallowleaf, raised with love and faith in the moon goddess. Choosing Selune requires sacrificing the Spear of Night and defying Shar's explicit command. It is the harder path mechanically and narratively, but it represents growth: she becomes a healer and guide rather than a weapon.

[Chunk 019] The darkness mechanic in Baldur's Gate 3 is directly tied to Shar's divine influence. Shar-aligned areas — particularly in Act Two — inflict the "blinded" condition on characters without darkvision or a Moon Lantern. Being blinded causes attacks to have disadvantage, skill checks to fail more often, and perception to drop to near zero. Players must actively manage light sources, position Torchbearer characters, or rely on racial darkvision to navigate effectively. The Moon Lantern, obtained from githyanki patrols or purchased at Last Light Inn, is the key tool: it projects an aura of Selune's light that negates shadow creature transformations and allows safe travel.

[Chunk 020] Shadow creatures are a unique enemy type found throughout the Shadow-Cursed Lands. They are former living beings — villagers, soldiers, animals — consumed by the curse and transformed into manifestations of pure darkness. They deal necrotic damage, drain strength, and can create additional shadow creatures from fallen enemies, making them dangerous to fight without area-clearing capabilities. Their AI prioritises dragging characters away from light sources. Players without a reliable light source will quickly become overwhelmed, as the shadow creatures multiply faster than they can be killed. Defeating them requires radiant damage, fire spells, or maintaining strong lantern coverage over the battlefield.

[Chunk 021] Companion alignment with Shar or Selune forms a key approval mechanic throughout Baldur's Gate 3. Shadowheart is the most prominent example, but other companions also respond to divine choices. Lae'zel, a githyanki warrior, is indifferent to both goddesses but respects power — she approves of using Netherstone power. Astarion, a vampire spawn, finds Shar's emphasis on darkness and secrecy darkly amusing. Wyll, the Warlock, disapproves strongly of Shar-aligned choices, as he is bound to a devil and understands the cost of dark bargains. Karlach, a tiefling barbarian, values warmth and life — she disapproves of any action that increases suffering.

[Chunk 022] Shadowheart is arguably the most complex companion in Baldur's Gate 3, and her arc is entirely structured around the Shar versus Selune conflict. Born Jenevelle Hallowleaf to Selunite parents, she was taken by Shar's clergy as a child and her memories erased through a dark ritual called the Rite of Thorns. She was remade as a Sharran cleric, indoctrinated with love for the Dark Lady, and given a mission. Her personal quest — "Daughter of Darkness" — spans all three acts and reveals her true identity layer by layer. She is simultaneously a victim of Shar's manipulation and an active agent of it, making her morally complex in ways few gaming companions achieve.

[Chunk 023] Companions who oppose Shar's influence provide countervailing moral weight throughout the story. Wyll actively seeks to protect innocents from dark powers and consistently opposes using Netherstone abilities. Halsin, the wild shape druid, was directly traumatised by the Shadow-Cursed Lands, having been trapped in Moonrise Towers for over a century. He carries deep hatred for Ketheric Thorm and what Shar's curse did to the Underdark ecosystem beneath the blighted lands. Halsin's questline — lifting the shadow curse — is one of the most satisfying in the game: it requires confronting a trapped spirit, freeing a child, and finally purging the land so moonlight can return.

[Chunk 024] Player choices accumulate throughout all three acts to determine multiple possible endings. Baldur's Gate 3 tracks hundreds of variables — companion approval, quest completion states, alliance choices — and feeds them into a branching endgame. Key decision points include: whether to use the Netherstones' power at all, whether to forge the Blood of Lathander weapon, whether to allow Shadowheart to kill her parents, and whether to destroy the Elder Brain or control it. Each decision shifts the balance between light and shadow in the final reckoning. The game avoids a single "good ending" — instead, different combinations of choices produce different forms of survival, sacrifice, and triumph.

[Chunk 025] The endings of Baldur's Gate 3 vary dramatically depending on Shar or Selune alignment. In the "full light" ending, Shadowheart chooses Selune, the shadow curse is lifted, Isobel and Aylin are reunited, and the world faces the Elder Brain without Netherstone corruption. In the "full darkness" ending, Shadowheart becomes Shar's Dark Justiciar, the player uses all Netherstone power, and the ending involves domination over the illithid network. Most players experience something in between — a compromise that reflects genuine moral ambiguity. The game's epilogue shows the long-term consequences of each choice, with different regions of Faerun recovering or deteriorating based on the player's decisions over three acts.

[Chunk 026] Defeating Shar's influence requires confronting her manifestations across all three acts rather than a single climactic battle. In Act Two, this means destroying Ketheric Thorm — her chosen champion — and freeing the Shadow-Cursed Lands from his anchor. In Act Three, it means confronting the Elder Brain, which the Netherstones would control, and deciding whether to destroy it or use it. Shar herself never appears in physical form during the main campaign — she operates through intermediaries, artifacts, and promises. This makes her a more insidious antagonist than a conventional boss: she is present in every choice her followers make, in every memory erased, and in every moment of hopelessness players encounter.

[Chunk 027] Restoring Selune's light to the Shadow-Cursed Lands is one of Act Two's primary quest objectives. The process involves multiple steps: obtaining a Moon Lantern to travel safely, locating the imprisoned nightsong — the immortal Aylin, daughter of Selune — and freeing her from Ketheric's ritual chamber. Aylin's freedom breaks Ketheric's immortality, allowing him to finally be defeated in combat. When she is freed and flies into the sky, her light begins to push back the shadow curse. Players witness the transformation in real time: the black sky lightens, the shadow creatures dissolve, and the ravaged landscape shows its first glimpse of moonlight in over a hundred years.

[Chunk 028] The tadpole infection is the narrative engine that drives player urgency throughout Baldur's Gate 3. The player character was infected during the Nautiloid crash in the prologue. The transformation into a mind flayer should take days, but something is delaying it — later revealed to be the presence of the Netherstones. The infection grants illithid powers but at the cost of gradual personality erosion. The tension is whether to use these powers and risk becoming the very monster you are trying to prevent. This mirrors the Shar-Selune dichotomy: easy power that costs you something essential, versus harder paths that preserve integrity but offer fewer shortcuts.

[Chunk 029] The moral corruption of using Netherstone power is reflected in companion reactions and narrative outcomes. Each time the player performs an "Illithid Wisdom" check — using tadpole powers — companions react differently. Lae'zel sees it as pragmatic power use. Shadowheart, if still Sharran, approves as it aligns with Shar's goals. Astarion approves — power is power. But Wyll and Karlach become increasingly uncomfortable. If the player uses the full slate of illithid powers, the ending options shift: more Netherstone-aligned paths become available, and the "resist the darkness" narratives become harder to access. The game uses mechanical progression to reflect ideological corruption, making every illithid ability feel like a small bargain.

[Chunk 030] Resisting the tadpole infection is the path aligned with Selune's ideals of self-determination and hope. Players who refuse illithid powers — even when tempted by their utility — find that their companions who value integrity approve. The Emperor, a mind flayer ally who guides the player in the Astral Prism, encourages power use. Resisting his guidance and the tadpole's growth represents a conscious choice of identity over convenience. The game rewards this path with a specific ending variant where the player emerges from the conflict unchanged — still themselves, not a mind flayer, not Shar's thrall. Selune's hope is ultimately about remaining who you are in the face of forces that profit from your dissolution.

[Chunk 031] Shar's doctrine is built on the sanctity of secrets. Her clergy — the Dark Justicars, the Night Masks, and lesser Sharran acolytes — are explicitly forbidden from revealing their faith to outsiders. Shadowheart's cover identity as a cleric of Selune is itself an expression of this: keeping her true faith secret is an act of worship. The theology holds that truth is a form of vulnerability, and to reveal yourself is to give others power over you. In practice, this creates deeply isolated individuals who cannot form genuine bonds. The game uses this doctrine to explain Shadowheart's emotional distance and her difficulty connecting with the party — she has been taught that intimacy is weakness.

[Chunk 032] Shar feeds on grief the way most gods feed on prayer. Her power grows when mortals mourn, when they lose loved ones, when empires fall and people surrender to despair. This is why she orchestrates loss rather than simply causing destruction — a destroyed village is forgotten quickly, but a village cursed to eternal mourning generates perpetual divine sustenance. In Baldur's Gate 3, Ketheric Thorm is the perfect example: a grieving father whose endless mourning for his daughter Isobel became the seed of the shadow curse. Shar transformed his grief into fuel for her agenda, weaponising his love into a force of destruction. She does not cause suffering by accident — it is harvest.

[Chunk 033] Selune's domain of navigation is expressed throughout Baldur's Gate 3 in subtle ways. The moon provides literal guidance — characters with the Selunite background trait receive bonuses to Survival checks at night. Sailors in the Baldur's Gate docks district often carry small Selunite medallions as protection charms. The stars that Selune governs serve as an orientational system for travellers in the Underdark, where no sun penetrates. Her followers believe that every lost traveller who looks up and finds a star is receiving Selune's direct blessing. This emphasis on finding your way — literally and metaphorically — reflects the goddess's deeper domain: hope as a navigational principle for souls lost in darkness.

[Chunk 034] Hope is the theological foundation of Selune's power and her primary weapon against Shar's despair. In game terms, this manifests as resistance mechanics: characters who resist corruption, despair effects, and shadow damage often do so through "hope" saves or through Selunite blessings. The Light cantrip — available to many clerics and some other classes — is treated narratively as a small invocation of Selune's domain. When Shadowheart eventually chooses light, she gains access to healing and radiant spells that directly counteract Shar's necrotic and shadow magic toolkit. The mechanical contrast between the two divine paths mirrors their thematic opposition: Shar's magic steals and drains, Selune's magic restores and reveals.

[Chunk 035] The divine conflict between Shar and Selune is ancient enough that neither side expects resolution within any mortal's lifetime. Both goddesses have existed since before the current age of gods. Both survived the Time of Troubles — the Spellplague — and numerous divine wars. Their conflict has outlasted civilisations, continents, and pantheons. In Baldur's Gate 3, the player's intervention does not end the war. It merely shifts the balance temporarily. The shadow curse can be lifted, but Shar still exists. Selune can be honoured, but darkness still falls every night. The ending is not about defeating darkness permanently — it is about choosing which side you stand with in an eternal struggle that will continue long after you are gone.

[Chunk 036] Faerun's landscape is marked by the physical scars of the Shar-Selune conflict. The Moonrise Towers themselves were built by Selunite monks who wanted a high vantage point for lunar observation and were later seized and corrupted. The Underdark contains passages where Shar's influence has driven all bioluminescence dark, leaving ancient drow settlements lightless. Ruins throughout the Shadow-Cursed Lands show stonework from two distinct eras: the Selunite prosperity before the curse and the post-curse abandonment. Archaeological layering tells the story better than any NPC: here was an altar to Selune, scorched and overturned; here were fields, now blackened; here were homes, now dissolved into shadow matter.

[Chunk 037] Magical light sources are essential survival tools throughout Baldur's Gate 3, particularly in Act Two's Shadow-Cursed Lands. The Moon Lantern is the primary tool: it casts a protective aura of Selune's radiance that prevents shadow creature transformation and negates the darkness penalty within a radius. Players can also cast Light, Dancing Lights, Faerie Fire, and Daylight to extend visibility and create tactical advantages in darkness-heavy encounters. Fire-based spells from Pyromancers and Tempest clerics are particularly effective, as they produce both damage and light. Selune's Spear, the Devotee's Mace, and the Coruscating Ring all provide additional radiant output, making them coveted items in a shadow-heavy campaign.

[Chunk 038] Darkvision is the racial adaptation that most directly counters Shar's shadow curse conditions. Races with innate darkvision — including drow, dwarves, half-orcs, tieflings, and gnomes — can see in magical darkness up to a specified range without suffering the blinded condition. In Baldur's Gate 3, Shadowheart as a half-elf has 60 feet of darkvision, making her uniquely suited to navigate Shar's domains despite serving the goddess. Lae'zel, as a githyanki, also has darkvision. Players who choose human or halfling characters lack this trait and must compensate through equipment or spells. The racial design reinforces the thematic point: some beings are more comfortable in darkness, and that comfort can be a trap.

[Chunk 039] Shar's quests reward stealth, misdirection, and secrecy. The Dark Justiciar trials hidden beneath the Gauntlet of Shar require players to move through shadow without triggering light-based traps, assassinate targets without alerting guards, and retrieve artifacts through subterfuge rather than direct combat. This contrasts with Selunite questlines, which typically involve revelation: uncovering hidden truths, exposing false identities, and bringing information to light. The Gauntlet of Shar is one of the game's most mechanically interesting dungeons because it punishes brute force and rewards patience. Completing it unlocks the Spear of Night — Shar's divine weapon — and advances Shadowheart's quest toward its dark resolution.

[Chunk 040] Selune's quests centre on protection, revelation, and reunion. Freeing Aylin from Ketheric's ritual, protecting Isobel at Last Light Inn from nightly assault, and ultimately reuniting the two lovers are all Selunite-aligned objectives. Uncovering Shadowheart's true identity is itself a Selunite act: bringing hidden truth to light. The side quest to restore the Moon Lanterns involves finding imprisoned pixies — creatures of Selune's fey-touched moonlight — and freeing them from their glass prisons. Every Selunite quest has the same structural DNA: something precious has been hidden or imprisoned by darkness, and it is your task to find it, restore it, and let it shine. This is Selune's gospel in mechanical form.
